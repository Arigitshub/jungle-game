# Jungle Game Design Document

## High concept

Jungle Game is a cinematic survival-adventure for Windows PC set in a hyper-real tropical rainforest. The player is pulled through the jungle by mystery, visual landmarks, environmental danger, and escalating psychological pressure.

## Player fantasy

The player should feel like an explorer surviving inside a living rainforest that is both breathtaking and hostile. The game is not about constant combat. It is about entering deeper terrain, reading danger, surviving uncertainty, and earning rare moments of awe.

## Pillars

### 1. Cinematic terrain

- Dense jungle traversal
- Waterfalls, cliffs, caves, ruins, riverbanks
- Dynamic rain, fog, shafts of light, and layered sound

### 2. Psychological pacing

- Tension and release loop
- Curiosity rewarded through discovery
- Survival stress balanced against visual relief

### 3. Premium immersion

- Minimal HUD
- Strong ambient audio and environmental storytelling
- Landmarks used instead of waypoint-heavy navigation

## Core loop

1. Enter a new biome or traversal pocket.
2. Read the environment for safety, resources, and routes.
3. Spend stamina, tools, and attention to push deeper.
4. Encounter a threat, puzzle, or navigation challenge.
5. Earn a reward: a vista, a hidden location, story clue, or resource cache.
6. Reset tension briefly and repeat at a higher emotional intensity.

## First prototype features

- First-person or close third-person traversal
- Sprint, crouch, climb, swim, jump, interact
- Basic survival meters: stamina and injury pressure
- Lightweight crafting or utility items
- One jungle predator encounter
- One ruin or relic discovery sequence
- Daylight, rain, and fog state transitions

## Systems

### Traversal

- Uneven ground and slope-aware movement
- Vines, ledges, water crossings, narrow cliff paths
- Traversal should feel tactile, not arcadey

### Survival

- Stamina pressure during escapes and climbs
- Limited healing or recovery resources
- Threat awareness through sound and line of sight

### Threat model

- Predators are more effective when implied than overused
- Human enemies should be added later, not in prototype one
- Threat escalation should come from uncertainty, sound, and visibility reduction

### Environmental storytelling

- Ruins, camp remains, broken tools, warning marks
- Audio cues before visual confirmation
- Story implied through space before direct exposition

## Visual direction

- Wet organic surfaces
- Heavy greens broken by warm sunlight and ruin stone
- Volumetric mist and atmospheric depth
- Camera moments that frame scale without taking control too often

## Audio direction

- Insects, birds, distant water, leaves, thunder, branches
- Dynamic mix should tighten under threat and widen during relief
- Silence should be used selectively to signal danger

## Success metrics for the vertical slice

- The player can describe at least one memorable landmark.
- The player feels tension before the threat appears.
- The ruin reveal feels earned.
- The traversal itself is enjoyable, not just the destination.
